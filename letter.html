iii · of vi
on noticing

there is a quiet that is not the absence of sound. it is the presence of attention — the kind a reader brings to a page they have no reason to read.

most of the world is not loud. most of it is waiting, arranged, patient. a kettle cooling. a window left ajar. a book dog-eared by a stranger on a train. these things keep their own time.

to live well, perhaps, is to learn the shape of this waiting. to not mistake stillness for nothing. to sit with the long hour between sunset and dark and call it, correctly, an evening.

iv · of vi
on doorways

most doors announce themselves. they have handles, hinges, a small brass plate. we walk through without thinking — the door has done the asking for us.

the better doors are harder to see. a gap in the hedge where the neighbour children slip through. a shortcut on a map nobody printed. a seam in the ordinary, only visible to someone standing very still.

i have come to believe that most of what is worth finding is reached by the second kind. not because the first kind is wrong, but because the first kind is shared — and the second kind, by its quietness, is almost always a gift meant for you.

v · of vi
on making

there are gardens kept by old men whom nobody visits. bowls turned by hand in a shed behind a house no one has been to. sentences polished, after midnight, long after the editor has gone home.

these things are not done for a reason we can name. they are done because the doing is itself the point — because to make a thing carefully is to say, without saying, i was here, and i paid attention.

true rewards are always deeply placed. you do not stumble into them. you arrive by being the kind of person who looks.
